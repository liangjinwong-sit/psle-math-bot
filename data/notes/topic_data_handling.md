# Topic: Data Handling

## Key Ideas
- Data can be shown in tables, picture graphs, bar graphs, and line graphs.
- A table organizes information clearly.
- A graph helps us compare values quickly.
- Mean and average have the same meaning in this topic.
- To find the mean, add all the values and divide by the number of values.
- Always read the labels, units, and scale carefully.
- Some questions ask for the total, while others ask for the mean or comparison.

## Standard Methods

### Method 1: Read a table or graph
1. Read the title.
2. Check the labels and units.
3. Find the value needed.
4. Compare values carefully.

Example: Read the heading and scale before choosing a number from the graph

### Method 2: Find the total
1. Add the values together.
2. Write the answer with the correct unit if needed.

Example: 4 + 6 + 8 = 18

### Method 3: Find the mean or average
1. Add all the values.
2. Count how many values there are.
3. Divide the total by the number of values.

Example: Mean of 6, 8, 10, and 12 = (6 + 8 + 10 + 12) ÷ 4 = 36 ÷ 4 = 9

### Method 4: Compare data values
1. Identify the two values being compared.
2. Subtract to find how much greater or less.

Example: 9 is 6 more than 3

### Method 5: Find the highest or lowest value
1. Read all values carefully.
2. Compare the numbers.
3. Identify the greatest or smallest one.

Example: In 5, 8, 3, and 6, the highest value is 8

## Question Patterns
- If the question asks for the total, add all the values.
- If the question asks for the mean or average, add the values and divide by how many values there are.
- If the question asks how much greater or less, subtract the smaller value from the larger value.
- If the question uses a table or graph, read the labels and scale before calculating.
- If the question asks for the highest or lowest value, compare all the numbers carefully.
- If the question asks for the number of items altogether, it is asking for the total.

## Alternative Phrasings
- average = mean
- total = altogether = sum
- greatest = largest = highest
- smallest = lowest = least
- how many more = difference = subtract
- compare = find the difference

## Common Pitfalls
- Forgetting to divide after finding the total for the mean.
- Dividing by the wrong number of values.
- Reading the wrong row or column in a table.
- Ignoring the scale on a graph.
- Giving an answer without the correct unit when one is needed.
- Mixing up total and average.

## Worked Example 1
Question: Find the mean of 6, 8, 10, and 12.

Step 1: Add the numbers: 6 + 8 + 10 + 12 = 36.
Step 2: Count how many numbers there are. There are 4 numbers.
Step 3: Divide 36 by 4.

Answer: 9

Why this works: The mean is the total shared equally among all the values.

## Worked Example 2
Question: A student scored 12, 15, and 18 in three tests. What is the average score?

Step 1: Add the scores: 12 + 15 + 18 = 45.
Step 2: There are 3 tests.
Step 3: Divide 45 by 3.

Answer: 15

Why this works: The average is the total score divided equally over the 3 tests.

## Worked Example 3
Question: A table shows the number of books read by four pupils: 3, 5, 7, and 5. How many books were read in total?

Step 1: Add the values: 3 + 5 + 7 + 5.
Step 2: 3 + 5 + 7 + 5 = 20.

Answer: 20 books

Why this works: The total is found by adding all the values together.

## Worked Example 4
Question: The values in a chart are 6, 9, 6, and 3. How much greater is 9 than 3?

Step 1: Identify the two values to compare: 9 and 3.
Step 2: Subtract the smaller value from the larger value.
Step 3: 9 - 3 = 6.

Answer: 6

Why this works: "How much greater" means find the difference.

## Worked Example 5
Question: Find the total of 5, 7, 9, and 4.

Step 1: Add the numbers together.
Step 2: 5 + 7 + 9 + 4 = 25.

Answer: 25

Why this works: The total is the sum of all the values.
