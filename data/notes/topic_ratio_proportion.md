# Topic: Ratio and Proportion

## Key Ideas
- A ratio compares two or more quantities.
- A proportion shows that two ratios are equal.
- Ratios can compare part to part or part to whole.
- Equivalent ratios have the same value.
- To share in a ratio, first find the total number of parts.
- In proportion questions, the quantities increase or decrease by the same multiplier.
- Always check what each part of the ratio represents.

## Standard Methods

### Method 1: Simplify a ratio
1. Write the ratio clearly.
2. Divide both terms by the greatest common factor.
3. Check that the ratio is in simplest form.

Example: 12:18 = 2:3

### Method 2: Find an equivalent ratio
1. Multiply or divide both terms by the same number.
2. Check that the comparison stays the same.

Example: 2:3 = 4:6

### Method 3: Share a quantity in a ratio
1. Add the ratio parts.
2. Divide the total quantity by the total number of parts.
3. Multiply by each ratio part.

Example: Share $20 in the ratio 1:3, total parts = 4, one part = $5, so shares are $5 and $15

### Method 4: Find one quantity from a ratio and total
1. Add the ratio parts.
2. Divide the total by the total parts to find one part.
3. Multiply by the required ratio number.

Example: Total 35 in ratio 2:5, total parts = 7, one part = 5

### Method 5: Solve proportion problems
1. Write the two equal ratios.
2. Find the multiplier from one ratio to the other.
3. Multiply or divide to find the missing value.

Example: If 3 books cost $12, then 6 books cost $24 because the number of books is multiplied by 2, so the cost is also multiplied by 2

## Question Patterns
- If the question asks to simplify a ratio, divide both terms by the same factor.
- If the question asks to share an amount in a ratio, find one part first.
- If the question gives a total and a ratio, add the ratio numbers before dividing.
- If the question says quantities are in proportion, use equal ratios.
- If units are different, change them into the same unit first.
- If the question uses words like compare, share, same rate, or equivalent, a ratio or proportion method may be needed.

## Alternative Phrasings
- compare = ratio
- simplest form = simplified ratio
- share in the ratio = divide according to the ratio
- same value = equivalent ratio
- one part = unit part
- total parts = sum of ratio numbers
- in proportion = equal ratios
- same multiplier = scale factor

## Common Pitfalls
- Adding or subtracting ratio terms instead of scaling them.
- Forgetting to simplify the final ratio.
- Sharing the total without finding one part first.
- Using different units in the same ratio.
- Mixing up which quantity matches each ratio term.
- Writing the ratio in the wrong order.
- Using addition when the question needs multiplication in a proportion.

## Worked Example 1
Question: Simplify 12:18.

Step 1: Find the greatest common factor of 12 and 18, which is 6.
Step 2: Divide both terms by 6.
Step 3: 12:18 = 2:3.

Answer: 2:3

Why this works: Dividing both terms by the same number keeps the comparison equal.

## Worked Example 2
Question: Share $24 in the ratio 1:3.

Step 1: Add the ratio parts: 1 + 3 = 4.
Step 2: Divide $24 by 4 to find one part: $6.
Step 3: Multiply by each ratio part: 1 part = $6, 3 parts = $18.

Answer: $6 and $18

Why this works: The ratio tells us how many equal parts the total is divided into.

## Worked Example 3
Question: The ratio of boys to girls is 2:5. There are 35 pupils in total. How many are girls?

Step 1: Add the ratio parts: 2 + 5 = 7.
Step 2: Divide 35 by 7 to find one part: 5.
Step 3: Girls = 5 parts = 5 × 5 = 25.

Answer: 25 girls

Why this works: The total is split into 7 equal ratio parts, and girls take 5 of them.

## Worked Example 4
Question: Write the ratio 8 cm to 40 cm in simplest form.

Step 1: Form the ratio 8:40.
Step 2: Divide both terms by 8.
Step 3: 8:40 = 1:5.

Answer: 1:5

Why this works: Both quantities are already in the same unit, so the ratio can be simplified directly.

## Worked Example 5
Question: 3 notebooks cost $12. How much do 6 notebooks cost?

Step 1: Compare 3 notebooks and 6 notebooks. The number of notebooks is multiplied by 2.
Step 2: Multiply the cost by the same number: 12 × 2 = 24.
Step 3: Write the unit.

Answer: $24

Why this works: In a proportion, both quantities change by the same multiplier.
